Incised Wound (Clean cut)

Caused by a clean surface cut from a sharp-edged object such as a knife. Blood vessels are cut straight across, leading to profuse bleeding. Structures such as tendons or nerves may be damaged.

×

Laceration

Blunt or ripping forces result in tears or lacerations. They bleed less profusely than incised wounds but may involve more tissue damage. Lacerations carry a high infection risk.

×

Abrasion (Graze)

A superficial wound where the topmost layers of skin are scraped off, leaving a raw and tender area. Often caused by a sliding fall or a friction burn. Foreign particles may be embedded, increasing the risk of infection.

×

Contusion (Bruise)

A blunt blow can rupture capillaries beneath the skin, causing blood to leak into tissues. Extensive contusions and swelling may indicate deeper damage, such as a fracture or internal injury.

×

Puncture Wound

Injury from a sharp object, like stepping on a nail or being pricked by a needle. These wounds have a small entry site but may cause deep internal damage and carry a high risk of infection.

×

Stab Wound

A deep incision caused by a sharp or bladed instrument, typically a knife. Stab wounds to the trunk are especially serious, with a high risk of damaging vital organs and causing life-threatening internal bleeding.

×

Gunshot Wound

Caused by a bullet or missile being driven into or through the body, resulting in serious internal injuries. A small, neat entry wound may be accompanied by a large, ragged exit wound, with a high risk of contamination.
